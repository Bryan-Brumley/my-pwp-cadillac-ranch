OUR STORY

In 1974, three artists from San Francisco found themselves in Potter County, Texas, burying ten Cadillacs nose first into a Texas wheat field alongside Interstate 40, an art installation that would eventually come to be known as Cadillac Ranch. Becoming one of the most celebrated roadside landmarks in the country. In 1974. People would stop along the highway, walk out to view the cars, then deface them or rip off pieces as souvenirs. Stanley Marsh 3 and The Ant Farm were tolerant of this public deconstruction of their art although it doomed the tail fins but they eventually came to encourage it. Despite the art being in an open field, for some, the Cadillac Ranch experience is very personal. Travelers near and far venture to view the public art and while visiting it, the piece seems to lend travelers a “sense of privacy and anonymity.” When stepping foot on the field, you’ll more than likely hear a mix of languages from French and German to British English being spoken, it’s one of the most linguistically diverse places to visit next to the United Nations, atleast until COVID that is. Cadillac Ranch is located on the south side of the I-40 Frontage Road between exits 60 and 62. It’s not particularly well marked, but Google Maps and GPS will provide you with adequate Cadillac Ranch directions. The line of parked cars and other visitors will lead you the rest of the way to this quirky roadside attraction in the middle of a Texas cow pasture. Cadillac Ranch is visible from the highway, and though located on private land, visiting it (by driving along a frontage road and entering the pasture by walking through an unlocked gate) is tacitly encouraged. rough an unlocked gate) is tacitly encouraged.
